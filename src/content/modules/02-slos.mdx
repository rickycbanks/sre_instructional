# Module 2: Reliability targets that change engineering behavior

Most services do not need 100% reliability. They need a **clear, measured target** that reflects what users actually care about. That target becomes useful when it changes behavior: teams release differently, alert differently, and prioritize reliability work differently once they can see error budget consumption.

## The three terms you need to distinguish cleanly

- **SLI:** what you measure
- **SLO:** the target you commit to internally
- **SLA:** the contract or promise with consequences

A crisp interview answer usually includes a concrete example:

> "Our availability SLI was successful checkout requests divided by valid checkout requests. The SLO was 99.9% over 28 days. We paged on burn rate against that objective rather than on raw CPU."

## Error budgets create an operating policy

If the SLO is 99.9%, the error budget is 0.1%. That budget is not a trivia fact; it is a decision tool.

- If the budget is healthy, the team can absorb more delivery risk.
- If the budget is burning fast, releases need more caution or a temporary freeze.
- If the budget is exhausted, reliability work becomes the priority.

That is the bridge between product velocity and operational discipline.

## Good alerts start with user-visible failure

Strong SRE candidates do not lead with "page on CPU > 80%." They lead with:

- user-visible failures
- latency that breaks the experience
- error-budget burn
- a signal that tells the responder what kind of action is likely needed

Infrastructure metrics still matter, but many of them belong in dashboards, tickets, or non-paging channels unless they clearly map to imminent user impact.

## Interview shortcut: how to answer "What makes a good alert?"

Start with:

1. It is **actionable**.
2. It is **user-centric**.
3. It is **low-noise enough** that responders trust it.
4. It is connected to an objective such as an SLO or error-budget burn.
